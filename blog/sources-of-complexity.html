Complexity and its many sources

I recently had an interesting conversation about what is and is not a complex AI solutions. It reminded me that we often deem something complex or not based on the aspect of the process or system we care about. I wish I could say I am an exception, but this conversation proved that I am not.

The conversation was about AI uses. Someone asked for complex uses of AI.

I got the question wrong and forwarded an article about how Auto-GPT helped me find new sustainable coffee brands that I have yet to test.

I find this usage case fascinating because the AI managed to do something very complex. It came one section short of doing an excellent job as a market researcher, entirely by itself, even if my input into the process was straightforward: a prompt.

Alas, the person was looking for something more like process automation examples. From their perspective (I think), a use case is complex when the implementer does something complicated.

I found this fascinating.

  • If one compares a prompt against the code needed to automate an industrial process, the code will likely be a more complex task.
  • If one compares the actions needed from AI to perform a repetitive process versus those required to deliver on an open-ended task, the open-ended challenge is likely the more complex feat.

Goes to show. Complexity can come from many different sources, and we are not necessarily considering them all in our daily thinking.
